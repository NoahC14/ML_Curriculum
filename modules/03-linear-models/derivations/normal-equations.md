## Purpose

This derivation develops the normal equations from two viewpoints:

- optimization: minimizing squared loss; and
- probability: maximizing likelihood under Gaussian noise.

The main lesson is that both derivations produce the same stationarity condition

$$
X^\top X \widehat{w} = X^\top y.
$$

## Setup and notation

Let

$$
X \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d}
$$

be the design matrix and

$$
y \in \mathbb{R}^n
$$

the target vector.
For a parameter vector

$$
w \in \mathbb{R}^d,
$$

the fitted values are

$$
\hat{y} = Xw.
$$

The least-squares objective is

$$
L(w) = \frac{1}{n}\|y - Xw\|_2^2.
$$

## 1. Optimization derivation

### Step 1. Expand the quadratic

Use the identity $\|v\|_2^2 = v^\top v$:

$$
L(w)
= \frac{1}{n}(y - Xw)^\top(y - Xw).
$$

Expand carefully:

$$
L(w)
= \frac{1}{n}\left(y^\top y - y^\top Xw - w^\top X^\top y + w^\top X^\top X w\right).
$$

Because $y^\top Xw$ is a scalar,

$$
y^\top Xw = (y^\top Xw)^\top = w^\top X^\top y,
$$

so the middle two terms are equal.
Hence

$$
L(w)
= \frac{1}{n}\left(y^\top y - 2w^\top X^\top y + w^\top X^\top X w\right).
$$

### Step 2. Differentiate with respect to $w$

Use the standard matrix derivatives:

$$
\nabla_w(y^\top y) = 0,
$$

$$
\nabla_w(w^\top X^\top y) = X^\top y,
$$

and, because $X^\top X$ is symmetric,

$$
\nabla_w(w^\top X^\top X w) = 2X^\top X w.
$$

Therefore

$$
\nabla_w L(w)
= \frac{1}{n}\left(-2X^\top y + 2X^\top X w\right)
= \frac{2}{n}(X^\top X w - X^\top y).
$$

### Step 3. Set the gradient to zero

At any minimizer $\widehat{w}$,

$$
\nabla_w L(\widehat{w}) = 0.
$$

So

$$
X^\top X \widehat{w} = X^\top y.
$$

These are the normal equations.

### Step 4. Solve when the Gram matrix is invertible

If the columns of $X$ are linearly independent, then $X^\top X$ is invertible and

$$
\widehat{w} = (X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top y.
$$

### Step 5. Interpret the stationarity condition geometrically

The residual vector is

$$
r = y - X\widehat{w}.
$$

Rewriting the normal equations gives

$$
X^\top r = 0.
$$

This means the residual is orthogonal to every column of $X$.
Therefore the fitted vector $X\widehat{w}$ is the orthogonal projection of $y$ onto $\operatorname{col}(X)$.

## 2. Probabilistic derivation via Gaussian noise

Now assume the response is generated by

$$
Y = Xw + \varepsilon,
$$

with

$$
\varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2 I_n).
$$

Then

$$
Y \mid X, w, \sigma^2 \sim \mathcal{N}(Xw, \sigma^2 I_n).
$$

For an observed target vector $y$, the likelihood is

$$
p(y \mid X, w, \sigma^2)
=
(2\pi\sigma^2)^{-n/2}
\exp\left(
-\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}(y - Xw)^\top(y - Xw)
\right).
$$

### Step 1. Take the log-likelihood

$$
\log p(y \mid X, w, \sigma^2)
=
-\frac{n}{2}\log(2\pi\sigma^2)
-\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}(y - Xw)^\top(y - Xw).
$$

### Step 2. Remove constants in $w$

For fixed $\sigma^2$, the term

$$
-\frac{n}{2}\log(2\pi\sigma^2)
$$

does not depend on $w$.
Therefore maximizing the log-likelihood over $w$ is equivalent to minimizing

$$
(y - Xw)^\top(y - Xw) = \|y - Xw\|_2^2.
$$

So the MLE solves exactly the least-squares problem.

### Step 3. Differentiate the log-likelihood directly

Differentiate with respect to $w$:

$$
\nabla_w \log p(y \mid X, w, \sigma^2)
=
-\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\nabla_w\bigl((y - Xw)^\top(y - Xw)\bigr).
$$

From the optimization derivation,

$$
\nabla_w\bigl((y - Xw)^\top(y - Xw)\bigr)
= 2(X^\top X w - X^\top y).
$$

Hence

$$
\nabla_w \log p(y \mid X, w, \sigma^2)
=
-\frac{1}{\sigma^2}(X^\top X w - X^\top y).
$$

Setting the score to zero gives

$$
X^\top X \widehat{w}_{\mathrm{MLE}} = X^\top y.
$$

So the MLE obeys the same normal equations.

## 3. Estimating the noise variance

Once $\widehat{w}$ is fixed, the likelihood can also be optimized over $\sigma^2$.
Substituting the residual sum of squares gives

$$
\widehat{\sigma}^2_{\mathrm{MLE}}
= \frac{1}{n}\|y - X\widehat{w}\|_2^2.
$$

This is the maximum likelihood estimate.
In classical statistics, an unbiased estimator under the correctly specified model uses

$$
\frac{1}{n-d}\|y - X\widehat{w}\|_2^2
$$

when $X$ has full column rank and the intercept has been accounted for appropriately.
The distinction matters because MLE and unbiasedness are different criteria.

## 4. Rank-deficient case

If $X^\top X$ is singular, the equation

$$
X^\top X w = X^\top y
$$

still characterizes least-squares minimizers, but there may be many such vectors.
In that case:

- the fitted value $Xw$ is unique;
- the coefficient vector need not be unique; and
- the pseudoinverse solution $X^+y$ gives the minimum-Euclidean-norm minimizer.

This is one reason the projection viewpoint is conceptually cleaner than focusing only on matrix inversion.

## 5. ML relevance

The normal equations matter because they unify several strands of the course:

- from Module 00, they use projection and orthogonality in linear algebra;
- from Module 01, they arise as first-order optimality conditions for a convex problem; and
- from Module 02, they show how a probabilistic model turns loss minimization into likelihood maximization.

The derivation is also the template for later modules.
Logistic regression changes the likelihood and loses the closed form.
Ridge keeps the quadratic structure but adds shrinkage.
Generalized linear models preserve the probabilistic viewpoint while changing the link between mean and predictors.
