## Purpose

These notes develop direct policy optimization for reinforcement learning.
The main thread runs from the objective $J(\theta)$ to the policy gradient theorem, REINFORCE, variance reduction with baselines, and actor-critic methods.
The note ends with brief overviews of A2C and PPO as practical descendants of the same core ideas.

## Learning objectives

After working through this note, you should be able to:

- define the performance objective for a parameterized policy;
- explain why the log-derivative trick makes policy gradients tractable;
- state and interpret the policy gradient theorem;
- derive the REINFORCE estimator from trajectory likelihoods;
- explain why baselines reduce variance without changing the gradient expectation;
- describe actor-critic methods as coupled policy and value learning; and
- explain, at a high level, how A2C and PPO stabilize policy optimization.

## 1. Why optimize policies directly?

Value-based methods seek a good policy indirectly by first estimating value functions.
This works well in discrete action spaces, but direct policy optimization has important advantages:

- it handles stochastic policies naturally;
- it works in continuous action spaces;
- it lets us optimize a differentiable policy parameterization end-to-end; and
- it avoids repeatedly computing $\arg\max_a Q(s,a)$ when action spaces are large.

In deep RL, the policy is often a neural network

$$
\pi_\theta(a\mid s),
$$

where $\theta \in \mathbb{R}^p$ are learnable parameters.

## 2. Objective and occupancy

Fix a start-state distribution $\rho_0$.
For a discounted infinite-horizon problem, a standard performance objective is

$$
J(\theta)
=
\mathbb{E}_{\tau \sim \pi_\theta}
\left[
\sum_{t=0}^\infty \gamma^t R_{t+1}
\right],
$$

where $\tau=(S_0,A_0,R_1,S_1,\dots)$ is a trajectory generated by policy $\pi_\theta$ and the environment dynamics.

The discounted state-occupancy measure under $\pi_\theta$ is

$$
d^{\pi_\theta}(s)
=
\sum_{t=0}^\infty \gamma^t \mathbb{P}(S_t=s \mid \pi_\theta).
$$

Using this measure, policy gradients can be written in terms of an expectation over visited states and chosen actions.

## 3. Trajectory probability and the score trick

The probability density of a trajectory is

$$
p_\theta(\tau)
=
\rho_0(s_0)\prod_{t=0}^\infty
\pi_\theta(a_t\mid s_t) P(s_{t+1}\mid s_t,a_t).
$$

Only the policy factors depend on $\theta$.
Taking logs,

$$
\log p_\theta(\tau)
=
\log \rho_0(s_0)
+
\sum_{t=0}^\infty \log \pi_\theta(a_t\mid s_t)
+
\sum_{t=0}^\infty \log P(s_{t+1}\mid s_t,a_t).
$$

Therefore,

$$
\nabla_\theta \log p_\theta(\tau)
=
\sum_{t=0}^\infty \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t\mid s_t).
$$

This identity removes the environment dynamics from the gradient expression.

## 4. REINFORCE

Differentiating the objective and applying the log-derivative trick gives

$$
\nabla_\theta J(\theta)
=
\mathbb{E}_{\tau\sim\pi_\theta}
\left[
\left(
\sum_{t=0}^\infty \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t\mid s_t)
\right)
\left(
\sum_{t=0}^\infty \gamma^t R_{t+1}
\right)
\right].
$$

A lower-variance and causally correct form assigns to each score term only the future return from that time onward:

$$
\nabla_\theta J(\theta)
=
\mathbb{E}_{\tau\sim\pi_\theta}
\left[
\sum_{t=0}^\infty
\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(A_t\mid S_t)\, G_t
\right].
$$

This yields the REINFORCE update

$$
\theta
\leftarrow
\theta + \alpha
\sum_{t=0}^\infty
\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(A_t\mid S_t)\, G_t.
$$

REINFORCE is unbiased but often has very high variance.

## 5. Baselines and advantages

Let $b(s)$ be any function of the state alone.
Then

$$
\mathbb{E}_{A_t\sim\pi_\theta(\cdot\mid s)}
\bigl[\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(A_t\mid s)\, b(s)\bigr]
= 0.
$$

So we may replace $G_t$ by $G_t - b(S_t)$ without changing the expected gradient:

$$
\nabla_\theta J(\theta)
=
\mathbb{E}
\left[
\sum_{t=0}^\infty
\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(A_t\mid S_t)\bigl(G_t-b(S_t)\bigr)
\right].
$$

The best-known baseline is the state-value function $V^\pi(s)$.
Then the quantity

$$
G_t - V^\pi(S_t)
$$

acts as an advantage estimate.

This is conceptually important:

- positive advantage means the sampled action was better than the policy average at that state;
- negative advantage means it was worse.

## 6. Policy gradient theorem

The policy gradient theorem states

$$
\nabla_\theta J(\theta)
=
\sum_s d^{\pi_\theta}(s)\sum_a
\nabla_\theta \pi_\theta(a\mid s)\,Q^{\pi_\theta}(s,a).
$$

Equivalently,

$$
\nabla_\theta J(\theta)
=
\mathbb{E}_{S\sim d^{\pi_\theta},\,A\sim\pi_\theta}
\bigl[
\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(A\mid S)\, Q^{\pi_\theta}(S,A)
\bigr].
$$

The theorem is powerful because it avoids differentiating the state distribution explicitly.
A full derivation appears in the accompanying derivation file.

## 7. Actor-critic methods

Policy gradients become much more practical when we estimate the return signal with a learned critic.

An actor-critic method has two coupled components:

- the actor, a policy $\pi_\theta(a\mid s)$;
- the critic, a value estimator such as $V_w(s)$ or $Q_w(s,a)$.

The critic supplies a lower-variance training signal to the actor.
The actor updates in the direction

$$
\theta \leftarrow \theta + \alpha_\theta
\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(A_t\mid S_t)\, \hat{A}_t,
$$

where $\hat{A}_t$ is an estimated advantage.

Meanwhile the critic is trained by regression, often on TD targets such as

$$
y_t = R_{t+1} + \gamma V_w(S_{t+1}).
$$

The critic loss can be written as

$$
L_{\text{critic}}(w)
=
\frac{1}{2}\bigl(V_w(S_t)-y_t\bigr)^2.
$$

## 8. TD actor-critic

A common one-step actor-critic uses the TD error

$$
\delta_t = R_{t+1} + \gamma V_w(S_{t+1}) - V_w(S_t)
$$

as the advantage estimate.

Then:

- actor update:
  $$
  \theta \leftarrow \theta + \alpha_\theta \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(A_t\mid S_t)\,\delta_t;
  $$
- critic update:
  $$
  w \leftarrow w - \alpha_w \nabla_w \frac{1}{2}\delta_t^2.
  $$

This combines policy optimization with bootstrapped value learning.

## 9. A2C overview

Advantage Actor-Critic (A2C) is a synchronous version of actor-critic.
The usual ingredients are:

- multiple rollout workers collecting trajectories in parallel;
- an $n$-step return or generalized advantage estimate;
- a policy loss using advantage estimates;
- a value loss for the critic; and
- an entropy bonus encouraging exploration.

A typical objective is

$$
L(\theta,w)
=
L_{\text{policy}}(\theta)
+
c_v L_{\text{value}}(w)
-
c_e H(\pi_\theta),
$$

where $H(\pi_\theta)$ is policy entropy.

A2C is conceptually simple and often used as a baseline in continuous-control and discrete-control tasks.

## 10. PPO overview

Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) is a practical policy-gradient method designed to make updates conservative.
Instead of taking an unrestricted policy step, PPO uses the probability ratio

$$
r_t(\theta)
=
\frac{\pi_\theta(A_t\mid S_t)}{\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(A_t\mid S_t)}
$$

and clips it inside a trust-like region:

$$
L^{\text{clip}}(\theta)
=
\mathbb{E}\left[
\min\bigl(
r_t(\theta)\hat{A}_t,\;
\operatorname{clip}(r_t(\theta),1-\varepsilon,1+\varepsilon)\hat{A}_t
\bigr)
\right].
$$

The clipping prevents very large policy changes from a single batch of data.
PPO is not a fundamentally different theorem; it is a stabilization strategy built on actor-critic and policy-gradient ideas.

## 11. Worked example: softmax policy on CartPole

Suppose a policy network outputs logits $z_\theta(s)\in\mathbb{R}^2$ for the two CartPole actions.
Then

$$
\pi_\theta(a\mid s)
=
\frac{\exp(z_\theta(s)_a)}{\sum_{a'} \exp(z_\theta(s)_{a'})}.
$$

If action $a=1$ is sampled in state $s$ and the estimated advantage is positive, gradient ascent increases $\log \pi_\theta(1\mid s)$.
If the advantage is negative, the update decreases that log-probability.

This is the basic actor mechanism in deep RL.

## 12. Common pitfalls

- Using raw returns without normalization in deep RL can produce unstable gradients.
- Forgetting that REINFORCE is on-policy leads to invalid data reuse.
- Treating the critic as a target value oracle: a poor critic can mislead the actor.
- Ignoring entropy regularization can cause premature policy collapse.
- Overselling PPO as theoretically exact: in practice it is a robust heuristic approximation.

## 13. Summary

Policy-gradient methods differentiate expected return with respect to policy parameters.
REINFORCE gives the unbiased foundation, baselines reduce variance, and actor-critic methods replace noisy returns with learned value estimates.
A2C and PPO are practical, modern instances of this framework.
