# Project: Unity Theory Synthesis Essay on a Canonical ML Topic

## Problem statement
Write a disciplined Unity Theory synthesis essay on one canonical ML topic such as optimization,
representation learning, sequence modeling, generalization, causality, or reinforcement learning.
The essay should begin from standard ML exposition, then introduce Unity Theory only as an
interpretive or research-generative layer.

## Allowed modes
Choose one mode and state it explicitly near the start:

- `interpretive correspondence`;
- `speculative research extension`.

Do not use Unity Theory language as a substitute for derivation, experiment, or standard notation.

## Suggested guiding questions
- What standard ML concept is being interpreted, and what is the canonical account?
- Which Unity Theory terms are being mapped onto which technical objects or processes?
- Where does the mapping clarify something real, and where does it fail?
- What new research question, if any, emerges from the mapping?

## Suggested readings
- The relevant core ML module for the chosen topic.
- Module 17 on Unity Theory perspectives.
- Card 10 companion materials and glossary notes when available.
- At least two standard ML sources that do not rely on Unity Theory framing.

## Recommended deliverables
- an `8-12` page essay written in publication style;
- a correspondence table marking exact mappings, partial analogies, and non-mappings;
- at least one worked example grounded in concrete ML mathematics or experiments; and
- a short appendix on limitations, objections, and falsifiability.

## Evaluation criteria
- the canonical ML account is accurate and comes first;
- Unity Theory claims are labeled as interpretive or speculative with precise mappings;
- the essay separates helpful synthesis from overreach;
- the writing could support a reading group or research discussion; and
- open questions are sharper at the end than they were at the beginning.

## Scope notes
- Essays that only restate Unity Theory vocabulary without technical contact should be scored low.
- A strong essay may conclude that the mapping is limited or unsuccessful.
